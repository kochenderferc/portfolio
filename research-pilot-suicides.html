Aviation · Mental Health

An Analysis of Factors in Pilot Aircraft-Assisted Suicides

Independent Research · Junior Science & Humanities Symposium — 2nd Place, Medicine/Health · 2022–2023

FAA medical requirements exist to keep aviation safe, but they can also push pilots to hide mental health struggles or avoid treatment, for fear of losing the medical certificate their hobby or career depends on. This project asks what factors actually show up in pilot aircraft-assisted suicides, so that policy can manage real risk while still giving pilots room to come forward honestly when they need help.

Method

  • Sourced from the NTSB's CAROL database (Case Analysis and Reporting Online), searched January 2023
  • Filtered to U.S. fatal cases where the probable cause explicitly indicated a suicide or intentional act
  • 43 aircraft-assisted suicide cases from 1989–2021, coded for certificate class, age, gender, medication, negative life events, and alcohol

Findings

  • All identified cases involved male pilots
  • Medical certificate classes spanned BasicMed through Class I, with nearly half holding a Class III certificate
  • Ethanol appeared in roughly a third of toxicology reports; medications were present in about a third of cases
  • Clear warning signs were common: many pilots spoke about wanting to die, and a notable share left a note
  • Relationship issues and recent criminal action were the most frequent negative life events
  • These patterns can inform future policy and legislation around pilot mental health.
